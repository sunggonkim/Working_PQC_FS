\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Methodology and claim scope}
\label{sec:eval_setup}

The evaluation follows the same spine as the design: RQ2 and RQ3 test the edge-runtime thesis, while RQ1, RQ4, and RQ5 bound the storage and recovery claims that make the placement result meaningful.  It answers five research questions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1: Correctness of authenticated publication.} Does the implemented format preserve nonce/generation, publication, and metadata-auth invariants under remount, fault, and tamper campaigns?
  \item \textbf{RQ2: CPU/GPU/PQC placement.} When should a UMA edge runtime keep AES-GCM file encryption on the CPU, and when can ML-KEM/cuPQC key-plane maintenance use the GPU?
  \item \textbf{RQ3: Mounted application behavior.} Does storage-visible admission recover SQLite latency under secure-storage pressure, and do scoped append-log/cache-manifest workloads survive remount?
  \item \textbf{RQ4: Replay and recovery oracle.} Which daemon/app crash and TPM replay-after-advance outcomes are supported, and which rollback or power-loss claims remain outside scope?
  \item \textbf{RQ5: Cost boundaries and sensitivity.} What strict-publication cost appears against mode-aligned baselines, and which controller/key-plane/anchor mechanisms account for the result?
\end{itemize}

The design/evaluation contract is one closure per main mechanism: CPU/GPU/PQC placement through data-plane negative controls and key-plane measurements; QoS through SQLite recovery and kernel-control comparisons; managed-memory scope through executor-local diagnostics; format through generation/tamper checks; D/J/C publication through oracle-labeled cut points; and TPM replay checks through replay-after-advance.

Primitive-placement numbers come from the microbenchmark harness; the mounted rekey result comes from the key-plane workflow harness.  Both refuse unsupported configurations rather than extrapolating missing rows.

The platform was an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Thor Developer Kit with 14 ARM CPU cores, a 1~TB WD PC SN5000S NVMe device, Linux~6.8.12-tegra, CUDA~13.0.48, liboqs~0.15.0, FUSE3~3.14.0, and cuPQC; it is not an Orin Nano result.  Retained O4 tegrastats logs give same-run power/thermal observations: mean VIN is 29.1~W for SQLite QoS, 27.5~W for key-plane rekey, and 28.4/27.4~W for AEGIS-Q/dm-crypt frozen rows, with max \texttt{tj}=49.0C.  These are observations, not energy-efficiency claims.

The strict frozen-contract row is a cost boundary, not the headline claim.  Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_workloads} states what each experiment can establish.  The frozen filesystem contract fixes 4~KiB 70/30 random read/write, \texttt{psync}, per-write \texttt{fdatasync}, queue depth~1, one client, a 1~GiB file, fixed mounts/platform, and five repetitions.  A same-profile attribution probe confirms that the strict path still pays data-sidecar, journal-sidecar, and marker/checkpoint durability boundaries per write-publication cycle.  The matched dm-crypt row runs on the same warm-cache contract: median throughput 9.03~MiB/s and median p99/p99.9 latency 264/823~\(\mu\)s; fscrypt remains environment-blocked because \texttt{CONFIG\_FS\_ENCRYPTION} is unset and the root ext4 encrypt feature is unavailable.

X6/O3 answer strict-path practicality without upgrading crash claims.  X6 changes marker/checkpoint publication from filesystem-wide \texttt{syncfs} to marker-file \texttt{fsync} (1,070 retained A2 calls), leaving data and journal barriers.  O3 keeps strict per-write publication as the D/J/C barrier boundary and admits hybrid epoch grouping only for concurrent or batched writes that can share a barrier.  The grouped row cuts four-client syncs from eight to six and improves p99 from 47.6 to 40.1~ms and throughput from 9.05 to 9.72~MiB/s; the fault-matrix row cuts syncs from eight to five but worsens p99 to 53.1~ms, so this is conditional admission, not a general fast path.  Single-client epoch still pays a data plus epoch-log barrier; kernel-assist remains future work.

\subsection{CPU for bulk data, GPU for batched ML-KEM}
\label{sec:eval_performance}

The diagnostic placement harness explains the key asymmetry behind Figure~\ref{fig:evaluation_summary}(c).  For a 16~MiB AES-256-GCM input, CPU/OpenSSL reaches 1.61~GB/s (observed 1.59--1.63~GB/s), whereas the managed-buffer GPU path reaches 0.172~GB/s (0.172--0.172~GB/s).  The GPU result includes managed-buffer staging and stream synchronization.  The retained 4~KiB--256~KiB data-plane negative-control rows amplify the same effect, with the current CUDA path 51--2061$\times$ slower than CPU.  This is why AEGIS-Q's data plane is CPU-first; a GPU label alone is not a performance result.

For a batch of 4,096 ML-KEM-768 key generations, liboqs on CPU reaches 64.8~K operations/s (64.3--64.9~K), while cuPQC reaches 1.50~M operations/s (1.49--1.51~M), a 23.2$\times$ ratio of medians.  The comparison is a primitive microbenchmark on one platform, not a claim that a FUSE write becomes 23.2$\times$ faster.  It supports the narrower policy of reserving GPU work for sufficiently large, independent key-plane batches.

The mounted ML-KEM-768 key-plane workflow opens 1,024 encrypted files and refreshes their HMAC-protected envelopes.  In the five-run methodology row, CPU-only refresh takes 24.575~ms (41.7~K files/s); with explicit slack, admission selects the GPU and finishes in 20.729~ms (49.4~K files/s), a 1.186$\times$ median speedup; with zero slack and high memory-pressure telemetry, fallback remains CPU-equivalent at 24.572~ms.  The X11 break-even model explains why the workflow gain is modest: primitive cuPQC is faster by batch~16, but mounted envelope/FUSE/admission overhead shifts the positive workflow break-even to roughly 760--812 refreshed files, and an added 5~ms of UMA or staging pressure shifts it to roughly 1.09--1.10K files.  This supports only maintenance-visible open-file envelope refresh, not hardware-backed credential release, a persistent KEM hierarchy, or non-storage foreground recovery.

These measurements produce the main placement rule used by the design: AES-GCM data writes are latency-sensitive and remain CPU-first, while ML-KEM batches are elastic and may use the GPU only when producer slack and telemetry permit it.  The result would be weaker if the paper reported only the faster GPU primitive; the important systems observation is the boundary between the data plane and the slack-tolerant maintenance plane.

\subsection{Correctness and negative controls}
\label{sec:eval_workloads}

The GPU integrity test passes leaf, Merkle, and empty-tree parity cases against OpenSSL.  The FUSE self-test covers journal/checkpoint/anchor units and a generation-replay regression.  The retained generation fault matrix covers partial update, clean remount, torn tails, stale snapshots, journal loss, older-generation append, and duplicate-generation replay across strict and epoch modes; every row has an oracle verdict without silent-corruption or unexpected-liveness outcomes.  X10 adds \texttt{UINT64\_MAX}/\texttt{EFBIG} near-wrap refusal, publish-ticket reservation, and same-file/disjoint 4-thread/4-process mounted writer stress, but not arbitrary application-level concurrency.  Clean remount byte-compares plaintext after \texttt{fsync}, and envelope tamper returns \texttt{EKEYREJECTED}.

The POSIX-scope audit records a narrow mounted-workload interface: ordinary read/write/\texttt{fsync}, scoped rename, directory \texttt{fsync}, no-open hard links, symlinks, open-fd truncate/fallocate, user xattrs, and bounded writer races pass without mixed plaintext.  Encrypted-file \texttt{mmap}/\texttt{msync}, open-subtree retargeting, arbitrary byte-range transactions, lower-filesystem xattr atomicity, and full crash-time POSIX visibility are rejected, scoped, or delegated.

Auxiliary storage/GPU diagnostics are deliberately scoped.  The host-pinning and managed-storage probes show same-buffer CPU/GPU checksum agreement plus CUDA pointer diagnostics, but they do not prove UVM migration suppression or final FUSE data-path DMA.

The file-backed anchor produces the deliberately negative result: restoring an entire backing-directory snapshot is classified as \texttt{rollback\_visible}, i.e., the previous committed baseline remains readable.  The result remains a negative control rather than rollback resistance.

The TPM runs record anchor latency, PCR reads, NV public attributes for index \texttt{0x01500010}, and a transient PCR-policy probe.  The hardware replay matrix labels stale disk/new TPM, new disk/stale TPM, missing index, changed PCRs, authorization failure, interrupted NV update, and normal replay-after-advance.  This is replay-after-advance evidence, not a claim that the persistent filesystem anchor itself is PCR-sealed.

The broader crash/replay matrices are selected-boundary evidence, not complete app-level crash coverage.  The daemon campaign kills the final binary at D/J/C, anchor, \texttt{fsync}, remount, and authenticated-read cut points; application rows cover SQLite and \texttt{dbm.dumb} TPM replay.  X1 adds three loopback ext4/device-mapper lower-block \texttt{error} rows; after \texttt{e2fsck}/remount, pre-commit rows recover the previous committed payload and the post-\texttt{fsync} row recovers the latest committed payload.  This is not physical power-loss, kernel-crash, drive-cache, arbitrary-workload, or multi-block raw-write certification.

The scoped mounted-application rows are not synthetic fio rows.  The secure append-log macro appends ordered 16~KiB records in strict and epoch modes, remounts, and verifies hashes/order; retained p99 is 5.93~ms strict and 4.17~ms epoch.  A cache-manifest workload publishes 24 hashed 16~KiB objects with closed-file rename plus directory \texttt{fsync}, remounts, and verifies all hashes, reporting 4.22/28.09~ms p50/p99 at 2.00~MiB/s.  The multi-writer row shows the queue-depth limit: epoch grouping reduces traced syncs from 16 to 1 and replay reconstruction remains sub-ms, but p99.9 worsens to 67.90~ms versus 61.54~ms.  These are scoped mounted patterns, not a broad workload suite or non-storage application QoS claim.

Recovery/replay outcomes support the mounted runtime boundary; they are not the paper's central QoS/placement result.

\subsection{QoS Recovery, Ablations, and Sensitivity}
\label{sec:eval_qos}

The tegrastats/CUPTI runs show platform pressure reaching the mounted daemon: they record 8 and 5 daemon-throttled flushes, respectively.  This remains wiring evidence rather than application-level p99 recovery or fast-notification bypass.

The app-level result uses SQLite transaction latency because it exercises the mounted file-encryption path, foreground tail latency, and elastic background pressure together.  Table~\ref{tab:qos_sqlite_recovery} reports five-run hero medians and three-run kernel QoS controls.  AEGIS-Q reports 8.15~ms p99, zero median misses, and 3.02~MB/s background progress; unthrottled storage reports 9.62~ms and 6.98~MB/s.  This is a Pareto point rather than a free throughput improvement: the simple controller reaches 7.54~ms p99 but only 1.50~MB/s background progress.  Kernel controls preserve background throughput but not the tail here: \texttt{ionice} reports 16.22~ms p99 with six misses, and \texttt{IOWeight} reports 12.57~ms p99 with seven misses.  This is bounded storage-visible control, not a uniqueness or non-storage QoS claim.

The sensitivity bundle varies budget, sampling interval, threshold, queue depth, and writer intensity.  The default storage-pressure controller uses 20~ms telemetry sampling, 0.70/0.60 enter/exit thresholds, and a two-sample hold; admission defaults are 4~KiB minimum GPU batches, 1.73~ms minimum producer slack, 100~\(\mu\)s deadline margin, 250~ms telemetry-age limit, and 0.80 queue threshold.  Baseline p99 is 6.4~ms; 80~ms sampling and 128~KiB chunks remain at 7.0/7.2~ms, high threshold disables throttling (0 sleeps, 8.8~ms), and two writers is fragile (12.3~ms, one miss).  No-throttle/no-slack fallbacks pass, and hysteresis records 9 transitions and 8 flips.  This keeps the claim on mounted storage-visible control, not an external application scheduler.

The ablation manifest then attributes cost to the authenticated FUSE format, SQLite recovery to elastic-write throttling, key-plane speedup to slack-gated GPU maintenance, and replay refusal to the external anchor.
